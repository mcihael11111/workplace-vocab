# Card Writing Guide
## Workplace Vocab: How to write every card

This document exists so that every card reads like it came from the same person. One voice, one standard, one level of quality across all 90+ terms. Before writing or editing any card, read this guide in full.

---

## The purpose of a card

Each card teaches someone a word they may have heard but never fully understood. The reader is a designer, product manager, developer, or researcher sitting in a meeting where this word just came up. They want to know what it means, why anyone would care, and how to use it without sounding like they googled it five minutes ago.

Write for that person. Not for a textbook. Not for an academic audience. For a capable professional who wants clarity.

---

## The five parts of every card

### 1. Name

The term as it is commonly used in professional conversations. Capitalise it as you would in a sentence. Do not add subtitles, taglines, or descriptors.

---

### 2. What it means

This is the definition. It should be precise, complete, and human.

The first sentence names the thing clearly. It answers the question: what is this? Subsequent sentences add context that makes the definition more useful. Think of it as explaining the term to a smart colleague who is new to the field. Not a junior. Not a student. A smart peer who just hasn't encountered this word yet.

**Length:** Two to four sentences. Long enough to be complete. Short enough to be read in under fifteen seconds.

**How to write it well:**

Start with what the thing actually is. Not where it comes from, not who invented it, not why it matters. What is it? Answer that first, then earn the right to add context.

Every definition should stand alone. If someone read only the definition and nothing else on the card, they should walk away with a correct understanding of the term.

Do not use the term itself in the first sentence of the definition. If you are defining "affinity map," do not open with "An affinity map is..." Instead, describe what it is without relying on the name to carry the meaning. This forces the writing to be genuinely explanatory.

Avoid vague openers. Phrases like "a method for," "a way of," or "a process that" are acceptable only when they are followed immediately by something specific. The word after the opener is where the real definition begins.

Do not pad the definition with history, origin stories, or comparisons to other tools unless those comparisons are the clearest way to explain the concept.

**Accuracy check:** Before finalising a definition, verify it against at least one credible external source. The definition should be consistent with how the term is used in the field, not just how it is commonly summarised online. If a term has multiple meanings depending on context, define the most relevant one and note the context.

---

### 3. Why it matters

This section answers a different question: so what? Why should anyone bother learning this word? What changes in their work or their conversations when they understand it properly?

This is not a second definition. It is a perspective on why the term has value.

**Length:** Two to four sentences. This should feel like the insight a senior practitioner would offer after years of using the term.

**How to write it well:**

Open with a contrast, a tension, or an observation. The best "why it matters" sections begin with something that reframes the reader's understanding slightly. For example: "Surveys tell you what people do. Interviews tell you why." That kind of structure shows the reader something they may not have considered.

Make it practical. The insight should connect directly to the work a product team does. Avoid generic statements about why understanding things is generally good. Be specific about what goes wrong when this term is misunderstood or ignored.

Do not repeat the definition. If the "why it matters" section could be mistaken for more definition, rewrite it. The two sections should feel meaningfully different in what they offer the reader.

Avoid superlatives. Do not describe anything as the most important, the single biggest, or the only way to achieve something. Make the case through the specificity of the observation, not through emphasis.

---

### 4. Example

This section shows the term working in a real context. Not a definition restated. Not a hypothetical. A concrete scenario that makes the concept tangible.

Think of it as a brief moment from the real world where this term is visible in action. It could be a product decision, a team situation, a design outcome, or a user behaviour. The purpose is to take the definition off the page and place it somewhere the reader can picture.

**Length:** Two to three sentences. Enough to paint a scene. Not so much that it becomes a case study.

**How to write it well:**

Ground it in something recognisable. Reference a familiar product, a common team situation, or a type of problem that most product people will have encountered. The reader should finish the example thinking "yes, I have seen that."

Do not invent statistics or attribute specific outcomes to specific companies unless they are well-documented facts. Keep examples plausible and grounded rather than precise and invented.

Do not repeat the definition. The example should add something new. If someone already understood the definition, the example should deepen that understanding by showing what it looks like in practice.

No em dashes. The same rule applies here as everywhere else on the card.

---

### 5. In real conversation

A single sentence, in quotation marks, showing exactly how someone would use this term in a professional setting.

This is the most practical part of the card. It gives the reader a script. A real sentence they could say in a meeting, in a Slack message, or in a design critique without sounding forced.

**Length:** One sentence. Always in quotation marks. Never more than one example.

**How to write it well:**

The sentence should feel like something a real person said. Not a textbook example. Not a perfectly constructed demonstration of the term. Something someone would actually say.

The best examples include a reason, a context, or a consequence. "Let's do X before Y because Z" is a stronger example than "We should use X here." The first shows the term being used with professional judgement. The second just uses the word.

The speaker should sound confident and specific. They are not explaining the term to the reader. They are using it naturally in a professional exchange.

Do not include the term in a way that feels performative. The sentence should not read like the sole purpose is to demonstrate the word. It should feel like a real moment from a real product conversation.

## Tone of voice

Every card is written in the same voice. That voice is:

Calm and confident. The writing knows what it is talking about without needing to prove it. It does not use intensifiers, exclamation points, or promotional language.

Clear before clever. If a sentence requires rereading, rewrite it. The goal is comprehension on the first pass. Clarity is the highest form of respect for the reader's time.

Specific over general. The writing earns its authority through precision, not through assertion. "Most users" is weaker than "five out of five participants." "Complex" is weaker than "twelve fields across four screens."

Human and direct. Short sentences are preferred. Long sentences are acceptable when the structure itself carries meaning. The rhythm should alternate naturally. Not every sentence needs to be punchy. But the writing should never feel dense or slow.

No filler. Every sentence should be doing something. If a sentence could be removed without any loss of meaning or clarity, remove it.

## What to avoid

These patterns appear consistently in poorly written cards. Check every card against this list before considering it finished.

**Circular definitions.** A definition that uses the term or a close synonym to explain itself teaches nothing. "Affordance is when an interface affords an action" is not a definition. Rewrite it from the outside in.

**False urgency.** Phrases like "in today's fast-paced digital landscape" or "more important than ever" are meaningless. If something matters, explain specifically why it matters. The context will speak for itself.

**Passive constructions used to avoid precision.** "It is often used to..." and "this can be seen in..." are ways of avoiding a clear claim. Make the claim. Own the sentence.

**Over-explanation in the example.** The in-real-conversation section is one sentence. It does not need a setup. It does not need a follow-up. One sentence, in quotation marks.

**Definitions that front-load process over meaning.** Starting with "a method for" or "a technique used to" delays the actual definition. Name what the thing is before explaining how it is used.

**Inconsistent capitalisation or punctuation.** Every card follows the same grammatical conventions. Terms are capitalised consistently. Sentences end with full stops. Quotation marks in the example section open and close correctly.

**Em dashes.** Do not use em dashes ( — ) anywhere in a card. Not in definitions. Not in the why it matters section. Not in the example. Em dashes are a known marker of AI-generated writing and they interrupt the natural flow of a sentence. If you feel the need to use one, the sentence needs to be rewritten. Split it into two sentences, use a comma, or reorder the clause so the thought flows without the pause. There are no exceptions to this rule.

---

## Cross-checking definitions

Every definition should be verified before it is finalised. This means checking the term against at least one authoritative source: Nielsen Norman Group, Interaction Design Foundation, or an established book in the field.

The definition on the card does not need to match a source word for word. It should, however, be consistent with how the field uses the term. If a source defines the term differently, understand why before deciding which version to use. When in doubt, define the term as it is most commonly used in a professional product team, not as it is defined in its most technical or academic form.

If a term has competing definitions, pick the most useful one for the audience and make sure the definition as written reflects that choice clearly.

## A complete example

**Name**
Affinity Map

**What it means**
A method for organising large amounts of qualitative data (sticky notes, observations, quotes) into themed clusters that reveal patterns and shared meaning across a research set.

**Why it matters**
Design decisions made without synthesis are just guesses. An affinity map is the bridge between collecting information and understanding it. It forces a team to agree on what they actually heard, not just what they felt they heard.

**Example**
A team runs twelve user interviews and walks away with pages of notes, recordings, and opinions. Without an affinity map, each person prioritises what resonated with them personally. With one, the team clusters the raw data together and realises that nine of twelve participants mentioned the same friction point in onboarding. That insight would not have surfaced from memory alone.

**In real conversation**
"Let's run an affinity mapping session before the next sprint so we can prioritise properly."

---

This is the standard. Every card should be able to sit next to this example without standing out for the wrong reasons. If a card reads differently (more formal, more vague, more promotional), rewrite it until it doesn't.
